# The Time Abram lived in Ur

## The society
1) Geography / Agriculture: Mesopotamia encompasses the land between the Euphrates and Tigris rivers. Its environment was low annual precipitation, increased temperatures, and limited water availability.  The geography of southern Mesopotamia is such that agriculture is possible only with irrigation and with good drainage.  With irrigation, the food supply in Mesopotamia was comparable to that of the Canadian prairies.

2) Law: Babylon was known as "the lawmaker" and created the Code of Hammurabi, which is one of the earliest sets of laws found and one of the best preserved examples of this type of document from ancient Mesopotamia.  Babylon became one of the main cities in Mesopotamia. 


3) Religion: Ur was a major center for Mesopotamian polytheism. Its patron deity was the moon god, Nanna (Sin). However, the region also heavily worshipped Inanna (later known as Ishtar), the Sumerian goddess of love, sex, beauty, and warfare. ## The socio-economic status

The Bible does not specifically state how Abram made a living while he lived in Ur. However, based on the biblical descriptions of his wealth, Jewish tradition, and the historical context of ancient Ur, we have a good picture of his socio-economic status.

1. He was part of a wealthy, semi-nomadic pastoral family  
The book of Genesis indicates that when Abram left Ur (and later Haran), he left with immense wealth. Genesis 12:5 says he took "all the possessions they had accumulated and the people they had acquired". Later in Genesis 13:2, it notes he was "very rich in livestock, in silver, and in gold." This indicates that Abram's primary livelihood was essentially as a wealthy herdsman or livestock owner. In the ancient Near East, a family of this status would manage massive herds of sheep, goats, and cattle, employing many servants to tend them.

Nimrod, king of Babylon, appointed Abrahm's father Terah, Chief General of his armed forces, making Terah the highest official in the land, beloved and respected by all.

2. Trade and Commerce
Ur of the Chaldeans was a highly advanced, prosperous Sumerian city located on the Euphrates River (in modern-day southern Iraq). It was a major hub for international trade, commerce, and agriculture. As the head of a wealthy pastoral family, Abram likely engaged in trade, selling livestock, wool, dairy, and meat to the urban center of Ur in exchange for silver, gold, and manufactured goods.

3. Jewish Tradition (Midrash)
While not in the Bible, ancient Jewish rabbinic tradition (the Midrash) claims that Abram's father, Terah, owned a business manufacturing and selling idols, as Ur was a center for polytheistic worship (specifically the moon god, Nanna). According to this tradition, Abram worked in his father's idol shop when he was young, though he famously mocked the customers and eventually smashed the idols.

Ancient Jewish traditions (Midrash) add that Terah was actually an idolatrous priest and a merchant who manufactured and sold idols.  The family would have lived in a society where sexual rites were normalized as part of religion. 4. Family: wife - Sarah, half-sister, no son

5. Abram left Haran at 75 years old.

In summary, biblically and historically, Abram made his living through large-scale livestock management and trade, acting as the wealthy patriarch of a large, semi-nomadic household on the outskirts of a highly civilized trade city.
